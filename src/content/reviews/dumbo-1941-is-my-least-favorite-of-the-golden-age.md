**Dumbo (1941)**. If you remember Snow White And The Seven Dwarves (1937), you may remember how revolutionary it was and successful it was at the box office. And how Pinocchio and Fantasia, both released in 1940, were bombs at the box office! Yes, they were well received, but the studio lives off of box office money, not movie quality. So they decided to take off an idea of Dumbo. Based on the book by Helen Aberson and Harold Pearl, so Disney had to use watercolor for the backgrounds. Then the animators disapproved of this idea, so they went on strike. While there was many tension, the film prevailed and hit at the box office. Now, I consider this to be the worst and most dated Disney animated film of the golden age. Now, this is still a great movie, it really is, but the other four were just a tad bit better. Why? **SPOILERS!**

**Story**

The story is executed great here, and even with its flaws, I believe it has stood the test of time. The flaws? Simple. One is that the story can be very racist and stereotypical. We only see a few black humans, and the film treats them as if they are jerks. That… is racism. Along with that, the film is incredibly mean spirited. The film has Dumbo figuratively go through heck, with the Elephants bullying him. All because he is a loving son. And honestly, why? Now, onto the timeless aspects! First off, the film is a real charm, in terms of comedy. I laughed quite a bit at the slapstick. That, and the film never does anything to really be different. But does it need to? It just needs to be a charming film that does the tropes well. That is exactly what the film does. But my favorite aspects are one: the message. The film teaches you that the very feature that makes you an outcast can become your greatest superpower. It really teaches you how to use your weaknesses to a strength. I have a different ability, so I totally relate to it. And then there is the heart of the story. I cried so much of the time, because it feels so human. Even 85 years later, the story is pretty good. **VERDICT: 15/20**

**Animation**

Dumbo’s animation is my favorite of the five aspects of the movie. First off, the color pallet is blue and gray. This perfectly affects the tone of the film: sadness and despair. But the movie has plenty of color, knowing that it is a Disney movie. The backgrounds are also great for the movie. Since the studio was in debt, the animators used not rich oil painting, but rather watercolors. I think that it works very well, and stands out from the previous three movies. Bill Tyrla did the character animation for young Dumbo. Dumbo cannot speak, so Tytla had to do his homework. And he got an A+, because I knew exactly what Dumbo was saying, without uttering a word. However, I think you know my favorite aspect of the movie’s look. It is the animation of the "Pink Elephants on Parade" sequence that I love the most. I did not as a child think that it was that good, just creepy. Fortunately, I came back to the movie, and loved it to the movie’s judgement for this scene. It looks exactly as creative as possible, and I cannot love it more. **VERDICT: 20/20**

**Characters**

Dumbo has a six-lead cast of characters, and I would say that they work, very well. Dumbo/ Jumbo Jr. is our main protagonist. He wants to win money, in order to save his mother from solitary confinement. The pros? This makes him very likable, and easy to root for. If my mother or father were put in solitary confinement, I would do anything to save them. The cons? Making him a baby and not making this a coming of age story was kind of the easy way out. This is because a baby doing all of this defies the movie’s own logic. Timothy Q. Mouse is his companion, who helps him follow his dreams. Now, Q. Mouse is my favorite character in the movie. He is the reminder to Dumbo that there is hope, and that is what I expect from movies like this. Mr. Stork is the messenger, and I got very charmed with his kindness. Jim Crow is another friend of Dumbo’s. He is easily my least favorite character of the movie. He is a key factor of the racism aspect, and I hate racism, especially in a kids’ movie! Mrs. Jumbo makes up for it, though, because she is the definition of important to the story. Dumbo is kind and wants to be a star, because of her, but she has the kindness to back it up. Lastly, The Ringmaster is the antagonist. And yeah, he absolutely sucks. He is a loud and boisterous moron, who you should be able to take seriously, but no. **VERDICT: 17/20**

**Acting**

Edward Brophy is Timothy Q. Mouse. Timothy Q. Mouse is supposed to sound happy and heartwarming, and Brophy knows that. He does exactly what you want him to do, and it sounds great! Cliff Edwards is Jim Crow, and he is a blast to listen to. Edward’s has such a vocal aesthetic, that makes him sound very humorous. It also helps that he has a lot of range for this role. Herman Bing is The antagonistic Ringmaster. Now, sure Bing’s character is a bumbling idiot, but he supports this. He sounds so humorous, but whenever he gets mad, it sounds real. For the last chapter as of right now, Verna Felton as Mrs. Jumbo and Sterling Holloway as Mr. Stork made their Disney debut in this movie. The two would be well known for being in Disney movies, and this would only be a well-done start, if both of them did a good job. Fortunately, that is entirely the case. **VERDICT: 20/20**

**Music**

"Look Out for Mr. Stork" is the opening number. Now sure, it sounds beautiful and heartfelt, but I often find it unnecessary. Like, what is the point, for this to be in the movie? "Casey Junior" is the upbeat song. Now, it sure is a bop, but feels inappropriately lighthearted song for a sad movie, like this. "Song of the Roustabouts" is probably my least favorite song in all of Disney, because of a very obvious reason. The song is incredibly racist, with the “Roustabouts” even calling each other “Apes”. Well, the good news is that the next and final three songs are all great. "Baby Mine" is Mrs. Jumbo’s song in her prison bars to her son. It is such a beautiful song, that really gives you a break from the drama and pain you endure, as you watch this movie. "Pink Elephants on Parade" is up next. Now sure, it was not very necessary to the story, but I could care more, since the animation looks gorgeous and very creative, and the song is appropriate to the film, and catchy. "When I See an Elephant Fly" is the final song, and it is supposed to be funny. But do not worry, it is! **VERDICT: 12/20**

**In conclusion**, Dumbo (1941) is the only movie of the golden age that I did not love. Now, sure, I will admit that I like this movie, but it comes so close to me officially loving it, but it just fell flat sometimes. So, do I find this animation at its peak? No. But will I admit that this is a well-done movie, despite and even with its flaws? Absolutely! **Final Score: 84%**
