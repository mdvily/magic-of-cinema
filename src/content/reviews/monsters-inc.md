# Monsters, Inc. Who would have thought Monsters tormenting a 30-Year-Old man, would make for an acclaimed buddy comedy? I had not a clue! But this film becomes one of the most acclaimed films in the very loved Pixar Golden Age (Toy Story 1 (1995) to the 3rd movie (2010). It’s a film fans and critics alike, argue have stood the test of time. But how good is it? Let us find out! SPOILERS!

# Story

# Right off the bat, the movie is simplistic, even for a Pixar movie. Monsters go into kids houses, via powered-up doors. They scare the kids to to get electricity for their world, in a company called Monsters, Incorporated: We Scare Because We Care, being led by Mr. Henry J. Waternoose III. But its simplicity is its superpower. It does not make us think. It just takes us on a fun journey. You never know what will happen, such as Roz being the director of the CDA, or Mr. Waternoose being the villain. We receive tiny clues that these plot twists could happen. For example, as the CDA comes in, Roz closes her office curtain, or Mr. Waternoose says he’d do anything to keep his company from going under. Every scene is perfectly paced, with an appropriate level of buildup. This movie is hilarious, because the comedy is always these one-liners that I receive a laugh out of every time. That never stops the heart, though. It is a film that has plenty of character scenes that are sad, due to the film building up to it. The message of friends getting into fights, but still being friends is executed well here, due to its power of how Sulley and Mike are different, which you will learn about later. VERDICT: 20/20

# Animation

# Many people consider the animation the weak link of the film, how weak of a link is it?** **The color pallet is all over the rainbow, but takes closer to shades blue, cyan and green. The action scenes are minimal, but the one action segment, as Randall chases Boo, Sulley and Mike through the door system, is very intense, yet fun. The art direction shows an appropriate level of attention to detail to the film. But the character designs and animations are the downfall of any animation criticisms, in my opinion. Since the characters are mostly monsters, the animation could look as creative as possible. Sulley walks calmly, and casually, like how he acts. Mike bounces around the room, due to his cheerful nature. Perfectly capturing the cold essence of Randall, he slithers around the room. VERDICT: 20/20

# Characters

# This cast is no Lion King, but they are lovable. Sulley, as the protagonist is selflessly lovable, due to his desire to protect Boo at all costs. The problem is that he has not the clue that he will never see her again. In the end, he learns to accept that she has grown up. Mike, though, is funny, yet selfish. He thinks he is so important, he has no necessity to file his paperwork, listen to his best friend Sulley or admit that he might be wrong. But he learns to accept Sulley for his differences, and be more responsible. Boo is adorible, and I love how the film sometimes shows, rather than tells you what’s going on with her. Celia is the love interest to Mike, who learns that he can deliver good deeds. Roz starts off as just funny, but has a smart twist in the end. And now, for the villains! Randall is a slimy, terrifyingly clever antagonist. He has known Sulley and Mike for quite some time, giving him quite an edge over them both. Mr. Waternoose is an effective twist villain, not only because not a soul can saw it coming first time, but he has known Sulley for quite some time. This makes the twist hurt. VERDICT: 20/20

# Voice Acting

# Every actor has a blast in this movie, and due to the film’s energy, that is a good thing. John Goodman portrays Sulley, with the character’s calm, warm and easygoing nature. Billy Crystal as Mike Wazowski is hillarious, energetic and full of life. Mary Gibbs as Boo is adorable. Jennifer Tilly is just like Celia: concerned and loving turned fierce. Bob Peterson as Roz: Humorous and tired, how the portrayal should be. Steve Buschemi as Randall Boggs is clever and scary, just like Boggs. Finally, just like Mr. Waternoose, James Coburn is worried about his company, while not wanting to kidnap kids, giving him depth. VERDICT: 20/20

# Music

# Randy Newman puts in three iconic scores: One is the main theme, full of life. Just like the movie. It is jazzy and fun. The second one is the scare floor, perfectly sounding like monsters doing their job to scare kids. The last one is the sweet lullaby-ish tune of Sulley and Boo together. Also, I love the song, “If I Didn’t Have You” by Crystal and Goodman. VERDICT: 20/20

# In conclusion, what more can you expect from Pixar’s fourth and near finest animated feature? It has a cohessive story, with funny comedy and powerful heart, beautiful animation, lovable characters, acting that never bores me and my personal favorite Newman music picture for the film. FINAL SCORE: 100
