**The Odyssey (2026)**. This was the first ever movie to be filmed using only IMAX cameras, and director Christopher Nolan made it so that the cameras were quieter and more maneuverable. This allowed Nolan and cinematographer Hoyte van Hoytema to capture the dialogue scenes on a sweeping, cinematic scale. Then, the film was filmed, no pun intended, in the course of 91 days on real seas and beaches and historical landscapes across the five countries Greece, Iceland, Morocco, Italy, and Scotland. Lastly, there is no main theme, because they wanted to make full use of the time period’s music. The film came out last Friday, and is being referred to as one of Christopher Nolan’s greatest films of all time. Now, that is a very high bar, because he is my favorite director of all time. So, has Christopher Nolan completed his odyssey on giving us the masterful Odyssey? Or is this movie’s incredibility stuck at sea with Odysseus? **SPOILERS!**

**Story**

Now, right off the bat, the film deviates from the 8th century poem by Homer. Athena is not a fan of Odysseus, but a reminder of his trauma. Odysseus is less cunning and intelligent, and more tragic and broken. The gods are removed from the movie. But that here is a good thing. It allows the movie to feel less of a copy and paste, and more original. I also love the horror sequences, especially the cyclops segment. As this happened, I held my breath in the theater, and had no idea if Odysseus and his men would survive. The sequences were very well done, and were actually built up to. Another thing I love about the storytelling is that our main sailor has to wrestle over his guilt. This gives the film a very real emotional anchor. But the movie’s intensity makes the stakes and scale feel very real. Yes, Odysseus’s journey back home happens too late, but it beyond makes up for it for two reasons. One: It makes time for Christopher Nolan’s non-linear storytelling. That is a very important aspect of his movies, so something would feel missing. Two: The ending exile is perfect. It wraps up the character arcs of Odysseus, Penelope and Telemachus so well. **VERDICT: 20/20**

**Visuals**

This film looks so beautiful, and unlike anything the directors and crew members have ever done before. First off, fans do sometimes think the film as gray-colored. I do not see it. I think that the film has two theme colors: red and blue. Red symbolizes Telemachus’ anger at the servants, and the blue is the massive scale. The audiences also say that with the cinematography, you can feel every important scene. I see that, perfectly. I really feel as if you can be overtaken by an incredible world. I was also overwhelmed by the body horror aspects, such as the men turning into pigs. The scenes make me think that the film looks so satisfying, yet so horrifying at the same time. But my favorite aspect of the film’s aesthetic is how there is very little CGI. This makes the film look very real. Christopher Nolan in that aspect went above and beyond, and he did not have to. **VERDICT: 20/20**

**Characters**

The characters are so well written, and instead of what most people would do and grade the characters, I want to go over why I love them all. Odysseus is our main protagonist. Now, while in the book, he is very cunning and intelligent, I love what they did here. Making a swords and sandals protagonist a broken figure sad and broken is very different from what a movie like this. Most swords and sandals main heroes are most focused on surviving and winning. Odysseus here is focused on his men that he has lost. That makes him feel very human. And his character arc of learning to let go and not suffer on their fates, just honor their legacy. His wife, Penelope learns to fight back against her disrespectful suitors. All while proving that girl power is a real thing. Telemachus, their son, learns to be a true leader, and not just a boy who does not know of his father. You get his anger at these traitors, and I love his determination to his father. Athena, Odysseus’ companion is different from her goddess form. She serves as a great metaphor to Odysseus’ struggles. And her not appearing in the end is the perfect showing not telling proof that Odysseus’ character arc is complete. Finally, Antinous is the villain of the movie. His disrespect to the kingdom of Ithaca is the perfect foil to Telemachus’ respect for his mother. **VERDICT: 20/20**

**Acting**

In all honesty, the acting here is perfect, and honestly my favorite aspect of the movie. Matt Damon is Odysseus. Damon sounds less like a cunning genius, and more like a broken man. That is perfect for his character here, and he moves like and sounds broken and depressed. That is perfect for his character. Anne Hathaway is Penelope. Hathaway in this role is graceful, yet has a personality to back herself up. Tom Holland is Telemachus. Holland is so timid at first. I love this, because it makes his character arc on becoming closer to being brave enough to stand up for his father feel even more earned. You really buy his attitude in the end, because it feels like a real person at their limit. When he declares the feast over before his father starts killing the servants, it feels real. Zendaya portrays Athena. Now normally, Zendaya would sound wise and nurturing. But here, she is basically a reminder of Odysseus’ trauma. She sounds like that, and her body movements do not disappoint. Now, while the supporting cast is great, the final main actor is Robert Pattinson as Antinous. Pattinson sounds so evil, but there is an amount of fear in him. **VERDICT: 20/20**

**Music**

The music was composed by Ludwig Göransson, and I find it wonderful here. Many people call it too loud, and frankly, I have no idea why. The music sounded the perfect volume to me. Another thing is that there is no main theme. Instead, to be more appropriate to the time period. And it works because Göransson knew how to make the music do the job right. This makes the film sound both creative and innovative. Not to mention, the film is great at sounding different from the rest of Nolan’s filmography. **VERDICT: 20/2**

**In conclusion**, Christopher Nolan has completed his odyssey when it comes to giving us The Odyssey (2026) and I am so glad he did. The Odyssey (2026) is a masterpiece, and as a fact, one of my favorite movies of the 2020 decade. If you are 15 or older, then you must see this movie, but if not, then you need to wait until you turn 15. **Final Score: 100%**
