# **Aladdin (1992)**. As the 31st movie of the Disney canon and 4th movie of the renaissance, this film is considered one of Disney’s best. Basically along with The Little Mermaid (1989), Beauty And The Beast (1991) and The Lion King (1994). However, was this considered some of the studio’s best, due to it changing the medium forever? No, because it was such a fun movie, one could not possibly get bored of watching even for the 10th time, from 1992, until now, 34 years after its release. Even Looney Tunes worker Chuck Jones considered this one of the funniest! It even has a TV show, 2 sequels and a broadway film, for crying out loud! But is this film as valuable as infinite wishes that can be anything, or does this feel like a cast and crew of street rats made it? SPOILERS!

# Story

# Aladdin, as I said, this did not change the film industry, it was just a blast. The story is very unpredictable, as yes, the film has a straightforward story, but it is the execution that gets me past it, as you never know what trouble Aladdin’s wishes will get him into. After he becomes a prince, who would have known that there would be a whole parade? Who knew that the film would give future references to Hercules (1997), Mulan (1998) and The Prince Of Egypt (1998)? Who would have guessed that Jafar would take the lamp, and actually succeed in the end for most of the climax? The story could have been predictable, yet it is the execution that prevents it from being so. The comedy is absolutely hilarious. Yes, the film’s slapstick can be on the nose, however, it is part of the characters’ personalities, whenever it happens, so it get points, rather than losing them. The line deliveries and lines period get a laugh out of me, due to them actually being part of the characters’ personalities. However, the heart is still a prominent and powerful section of the movie, which, along with the message of you can turn your life around, mostly resides in Aladdin’s character growth. My only real gripes are that the romance with Jasmine is unnecessary, and the pacing is a bit too fast for my taste. However, if you can get past that, the story is one to check out. VERDICT: 17/20

# Animation

# You might have noticed a few criticisms to the story of the movie, even is I thought it was very well done. Fortunately, with the animation, that is not the case. The animation is bright, energetic and immersive in a way most other movies cannot match. First off, the color is off the charts, mostly taking shades of yellow and orange, with a little bit of blue, perfectly reflecting where this film is: Saudi Arabia. I also love how the color extrudes, while not as expressive, are still made beautifully, from the Sultan’s Palace, to the insides of the strict Cave Of Wonders. The action scene in the end is extremely engaging, not only narratively, but visually, in terms of undying creativity. However, it is the character designs and animation I love the most, as they are fluid, and creative in a way where one can think it is not possible. VERDICT: 20/20

# Characters

# So many characters here are complete delights. Let us begin with our protagonist, Aladdin. Aladdin is one of the most compelling heroes in all of Disney, from his character development, to his relatability, making us have to root for him, even when he breaks the rules, in a way where we cannot with other Disney protagonists. Part of him wants to be a kind soul to others, the other wants a better life for himself. The Genie is such a fun character, and extremely likable, as he acts like one of Aladdin’s companions, with such a heart that makes the film have a kinder side. Abu, The Magic Carpet and Rajah are companions with such distinct personalities, making them never feel interchangeable. Jafar is the titular antagonist, and his ways of foiling the idiotic Sultan, even considering his lord’s low intelligence is smart, but when he gets the lamp, he becomes terrifying and a real challenge for the “Street Rat”, with him using his intelligence in a believable way is a great message: Use your brain, not your brawn. My only real criticisms are that The Sultan is such a stupid character for no reason, and Jasmine is not a bad character, as she has a motive you understand, but her character arc? Could use some work. VERDICT: 17/20

# Acting

# The acting is some of the best of the entire Disney canon, which is a very high bar. Scott Weinger is Aladdin with his undying charm, along with his desire to do the right thing, making him feel like a character you want to root for. Linda Larkin is Jasmine, with you really getting her desire to be treated like her own person. Jonathan Freeman is intimidating and menacing as the vizier Jafar, without falling into the loud and boisterous trap. Frank Welker is Abu, The Cave and Rajah, and if you play 3 characters in one movie, you have my respect, if you do a good job, which Welker does. Douglas Seale as Agrabah’s Sultan is charming, humorous and full of life, in a way where I had no idea was even possible in the first place, due to his character. Gilbert Gottfried is Iago, with a humorous voice and one of the best ever villain/henchman with Freeman’s Jafar, giving the film such charm. However, none of them even remotely compare to the unfortunately deceased Robin Williams’ Genie, who could not have nor energy, without being annoying. VERDICT: 20/20

# Music

# Aladdin’s score is one of Disney’s best, with such energy for the strong scenes, while the softer scenes feel more melodic, which is obviously a good thing. “Arabian Nights“ is a perfect way to mystique the world of Agrabah, and Disney’s depiction of Saudi Arabia, in a way that feels natural, yet grand. "One Jump Ahead" and "One Jump Ahead (Reprise)" are perfect reads into Aladdin and Abu’s characters, showing us who they truly are: Unfortunate “Street Rats”, who desire better lives. "Prince Ali" showcases the aftermath of Aladdin’s first wish, presenting the character’s alter ego of Prince Ali, with a grand purpose to the story. “A Whole New World” is a ballad between Aladdin showing Jasmine a free world, and Jasmine discovering how there is more to life than just being inside the palace walls. "Prince Ali (Reprise)" is Jafar exposing and banishing Aladdin, with lyrics that support him becoming from a great to a fantastic antagonist. However, in terms of sheer fun and quality, you just cannot dislike is the Genie’s description song, "Friend Like Me". VERDICT: 20/20

# To conclude Aladdin is an entertaining classic. It takes every generic trope from a Disney movie, and not only subverts, yet transcends it, with its undying and diligent quality. I would make a recommendation to Disney’s masterpiece of a 31st animated feature, but I have the feeling you already have seen it, making said recommendation factually seem unnecessary. Final Score: 94%
