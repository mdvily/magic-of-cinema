**One Hundred and One Dalmatians (1961)**. Disney’s adaptation of the 1956 novel by Dodie Smith was not as easy to make you might expect it would be. First, the crew at Disney had to make 6.4 million spots, which was impossible. So they did what we refer to as the xerox method. Then, in order to make the story more simplistic, writer Bill Peet turned the two Dalmatian mothers into one. On top of that, he also made the henchmen more simple. Lastly, Peet took the setting into mid-century London. This movie is very well received, with Rotten Tomatoes giving it 98%, and even IMDb was quite kind to it, with it getting a rating of 7.3/10. But is this really that good of a movie? **SPOILERS!**

**Story**

The story is about this: A dalmatian father Pongo, and dalmatian mother Perdita have 15 puppies, after falling in love. Cruella De Vil, the antagonist, wants to use the fur for their coats. It now is up to the parents to rescue their children. One thing I love about the film is that parents trying to save their children always has the potential to be very heartfelt. And here, the heart does not disappoint. 

**Acting**

Rod Taylor and Cate Bauer are the parents Pongo and Perdita. Taylor and Bauer have such excellent chemistry with each other, you start to feel like they worked together. On top of that, I love how Taylor has more energy and excitement, all while Bauer has more motherly power to their young. This really gives them a vibe. Lisa Davis and Ben Wright as Anita and Roger are the exact same deal. The difference? They sound even more so than the dogs. My only real gripe is that the puppies all have the exact same tone. This stops the world from feeling as lived in. David Frankham portrays Tibbs. Frankham as Tibbs is so caring, and has such excellent chemistry with J. Pat O'Malley‘s the colonel. J. Pat O'Malley, and Frederick Worlock were the henchmen Jasper and Horace. They sound so hilarious, and have the chemistry with Betty Lou Gerson’s Cruella. Speaking of which, Gerson delivers the greatest portrayal of them all. She commands the screen, with such calculating lines. **VERDICT: 18/20**

**Music**

The music is great here, I just wish that they could sound less authentic all the time, and more peaceful when need be. "Overture," "A Beautiful Spring Day," and "What's All the Hurry / A Perfect Situation / Stir Things Up" are my personal favorites. They help set up the story so well, and it really sounds like it. "Cruella De Vil" is a humorous song, and Roger playing the piano as he sings it is pretty funny for me. "Sergeant Tibs' Recon", "Can You Leave Tonight?", "Pulling a Snitch", "My Darlings", “Through The Snow”, "I'm Hungry” "Dinsford", and "Puppies Everywhere" are the times when sounding so authentic is a mixed thing. Sometimes, like “Through The Snow” it is a good thing, because it understands the severity of the sequences. But, "I'm Hungry” is not. Why? Because it makes us think that the scene is more powerful than it actually is. "Dalmatian Plantation / Finale" however, is the most satisfying. The music also does not disappoint, with sounding charming and peaceful, at last. **VERDICT: 18/20**
