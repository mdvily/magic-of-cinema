# **One Hundred and One Dalmatians (1961)**. Disney’s adaptation of the 1956 novel by Dodie Smith was not as easy to make you might expect it would be. First, the crew at Disney had to make 6.4 million spots, which was impossible. So they did what we refer to as the xerox method. Then, in order to make the story more simplistic, writer Bill Peet turned the two Dalmatian mothers into one. On top of that, he also made the henchmen more simple. Lastly, Peet took the setting into mid-century London. This movie is very well received, with Rotten Tomatoes giving it 98%, and even IMDb was quite kind to it, with it getting a rating of 7.3/10. But is this really that good of a movie? **SPOILERS! **

# **Story**

# The story is about this: A dalmatian father Pongo, and dalmatian mother Perdita have 15 puppies, after falling in love. Cruella De Vil, the antagonist, wants to use the fur for their coats. It now is up to the parents to rescue their children. One thing I love about the film is that parents trying to save their children always has the potential to be very heartfelt. And here, the heart does not disappoint. We all see so many scenes where the dogs actually question if they can save the pups. This adds on to the scenes where you actually shed a tear. The message on he.ping those who have worse situations than you. That is an incredible message, that can turn you into a better person. It teaches you to show care for other people. My favorite aspect of the movie, however, is the pacing. Every scene is paced so well, and it never once feels boring. I got invested in every single scene, because it had an appropriate amount of buildup. My only real gripe is how annoying the comedy is. I never once laughed, because it is too slapsticky for my taste. **VERDICT: 16/20**

# **Animation**

# The movie looks absolutely beautiful, in so many aspects. I might as well start with my only real criticism: the color for most of the movie. The color pallet is fine, but it never really has that much of a vibe. Most of the film just looks black, white and gray. But everything else looks absolutely beautiful. Now, onto my many positives of the movie’s look. First off, the animation style is more blocky and geometric than other movies. It is a very refreshing change of pace, and I love how much it differs. The character designs are also great. Every character looks absolutely beautiful, and very much like their respective animal, be it human, or pet. The animation of said characters is even better. It looks incredibly fluid, from beginning to end. First watching this, at five, I did not expect Roger to have such incredible and lively character animation. I must also praise the background imagery. They look so different from other Disney movies, and that is to praise. This is because it has a very real vibe. **VERDICT: 19/20**

# **Characters**

# The cast of characters is absolutely lovable, and fun to watch. I might as well start off with Pongo, the puppies’ father, and Perdita, the mother. I love respectively, how the film takes its time to establish the relationship. The movie could have just had them be married from the start, but no. The film starts off with them not really knowing each other, and they get children at an appropriate pace. But still, I grew attached to them very quickly, and was surprised how much I wanted them to save the puppies. The puppies themselves are an all mixed bag. They are very adorable, sure, but I honestly could not tell which one was which one. I guess it would work better if they had different personalities. This blocks the world from feeling very lived in. Roger and Anita, however? I feel like they were just in the movie, just to be in the movie. They do very little to enhance the plot, very much. Sergeant Tibbs and the Colonel, however? They actually did, and I was surprised how likable they were. They had such different personalities, but they both want to do the right thing, for the right reason. This makes them very likable. Jasper and Horace are the henchmen. They both are so incompetent, and funny. At the same time, however, they can be scary, when need be. Cruella, however, is the greatest part of the movie. She wants the puppies, just to make them into a fur coat. Who does that? But she also does not disappoint in sheer ruthlessness. **VERDICT: 17/20**

# **Acting**

# Rod Taylor and Cate Bauer are the parents Pongo and Perdita. Taylor and Bauer have such excellent chemistry with each other, you start to feel like they worked together. On top of that, I love how Taylor has more energy and excitement, all while Bauer has more motherly power to their young. This really gives them a vibe. Lisa Davis and Ben Wright as Anita and Roger are the exact same deal. The difference? They sound even more so than the dogs. My only real gripe is that the puppies all have the exact same tone. This stops the world from feeling as lived in. David Frankham portrays Tibbs. Frankham as Tibbs is so caring, and has such excellent chemistry with J. Pat O'Malley‘s the colonel. J. Pat O'Malley, and Frederick Worlock were the henchmen Jasper and Horace. They sound so hilarious, and have the chemistry with Betty Lou Gerson’s Cruella. Speaking of which, Gerson delivers the greatest portrayal of them all. She commands the screen, with such calculating lines. **VERDICT: 17/20**

# **Music**

# The music is great here, I just wish that they could sound less authentic all the time, and more peaceful when need be. "Overture," "A Beautiful Spring Day," and "What's All the Hurry / A Perfect Situation / Stir Things Up" are my personal favorites. They help set up the story so well, and it really sounds like it. "Cruella De Vil" is a humorous song, and Roger playing the piano as he sings it is pretty funny for me. "Sergeant Tibs' Recon", "Can You Leave Tonight?", "Pulling a Snitch", "My Darlings", “Through The Snow”, "I'm Hungry” "Dinsford", and "Puppies Everywhere" are the times when sounding so authentic is a mixed thing. Sometimes, like “Through The Snow” it is a good thing, because it understands the severity of the sequences. But, "I'm Hungry” is not. Why? Because it makes us think that the scene is more powerful than it actually is. "Dalmatian Plantation / Finale" however, is the most satisfying. The music also does not disappoint, with sounding charming and peaceful, at last. **VERDICT: 18/20**

# **To conclude**, One Hundred and One Dalmatians (1961) is a delight of a movie. It is so charming, and sweet, and the amount of effort in the story, animation, characters, acting and music is very admirable. Sure, it may have a couple of shortcomings, but it turns it around. It should therefore come as no surprise that I would recommend this to any avid Disney fan. **Final Score: 87%**
