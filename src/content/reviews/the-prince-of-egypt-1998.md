# **The Prince Of Egypt (1998)**. In his career of working for Disney, Jeffrey Katzenberg had always wanted to make a biblical movie, but Michael Eisner had rejected his offer. Therefore, Katzenberg quit the studio in order to make his project come to life, ergo, starting his new studio DreamWorks. Now, biblical movies are not always considered the most acclaimed animated feature films of all time, especially as the most remembered piece of biblical animation is Veggie Tales, take The Star (2017) for example. On the flipside, The Prince Of Egypt is considered a film that is… different and stands above the rest. Yet, is this film as wonderful as the parting of the Red Sea, or as atrocious as the Plagues Of Egypt? SPOILERS!

# Story

# Right off the bat, DreamWorks took creative liberties from the Jewish book of Exodus, but toned it down in order to make this kid friendly and deserving of a PG (Parental Guidance Recommended) age rating. And they absolutely did the job right! First off, one thing I love about the story is that it takes its time to perfectly establish the story of how the Israelites escaped the Egyptians, and received a better life. Now, a criticism I have heard is quite some many times of the film is that the slight humorous segments do not blend well with the tone, however, I must respectfully disagree for three reasons: 1; The comedy is done in short nuanced ways, 2; it is a film that is actually funny when need be, and 3; it never kills the film’s overall tone, as the movie gives slight breaks to not traumatize the audiences. However, what I love the most I also love is the drama, as the movie would take its time to fully establish Moses and Rameses’ brotherhood, before they become unfortunate enemies, making the tension feel earned and believable. The film starts off as something enjoyable, and entertaining, however, this film becomes one of Animation’s not only finest, but greatest of all time, as God recruits Moses for the task of freeing the Jews forever. Also, this film gets incredibly dark, from Moses man slaughtering his own nephew, to an infanticide at the beginning. I finally love how this film is so enjoyable, yet so powerful, that anyone can enjoy it, Jewish or not. VERDICT: 20/20

# Animation

# The film would normally stick to colors, yellow, orange, brown and gold, which perfectly reflect of the heroic tone, as well as the location of the movie: Egypt. The textures look exactly like Ancient Egypt, such as the Pharaoh’s palace, and the Jewish huts. The character animation and designs looks so accurate even 28 years after the film’s release. However, the thing I love most about the film’s visual magic is that of the special effects. They look like they were made of God, due to them doing things most animated films would not dream of, from the sea of blood, to God being like a spirit through the air. VERDICT: 20/20

# Characters

# Moses is our main protagonist, and Moses is a hero many can relate to, as while he works so hard to save his people, he becomes both an outcast to Egypt and to his brother, as I especially love how much the brothers from the start spend time together, making their falling-out feel real. Speaking of which, Rameses is one of the most complex antagonists in an animated feature, as he both wants to make proud his father Seti, all while wanting back his brother. God is very crucial to the story, as he is the one who hires Moses for this job, however, a prominent character trait he has is believing in Moses, making his journey more satisfying by the end. Zipporah, Moses’ wife is both proud of her husband, all while being worried about her husband, signifying a prominent character arc of learning that her husband is making the right choice. Miriam, Moses’s sister, is the one to reveal to her brother that he is a Jew, giving Moses a driving force throughout the picture. Aaron, his brother, and the the older son of Yocheved, is more skeptical of Moses, and for good reason, too: As Moses works hard to free the Jews, Pharaoh makes the choice to punish the Jews, making Aaron more upset, yet I love his character arc of learning to accept his younger brother for his attempts to save his people. Hotep and Huy are the creepy henchmen, in the most fun and entertaining way. Finally, Jethro, Zipporah‘s father, is the first to praise his son-in-law for his bravery, being the driving force of my favorite scene. VERDICT: 20/20

# Acting

# Val Kilmer is Moses, with both raw, unadulterated power to his voice, along with such sincerity to Moses’s character. Ralph Fiennes is Pharaoh Rameses II, with both his pride and stubbornness, as well as genuine regret to never seeing his younger brother again. Kilmer also voices God itself, and instead of just generically being loud and boisterous, Kilmer brings a soft power into God. Michelle Pfeiffer is Zipporah, with amazing chemistry with Kilmer, as well as such emotion. Jeff Goldblum ( Who actually met my maternal uncle) plays Aaron, and while Goldblum is known for stuttering roles, due to Aaron’s skepticism of his younger brother. Sandra Bullock portrays Miriam, perfectly capturing the concern and love for her younger brother, Moses. Steve Martin and Martin Short are priests Hotep and Huy, which was a tricky choice, due to them both being loved comedians, but I do love how the Martins put in a lot of effort and respect into their characters. Sir Patrick Stewart is Pharaoh Seti I, with his commanding presence, as well as as concern for both of his sons. Helen Mirren is the Queen, with Mirren lending in her soft voice for her sons very well. Finally, Daniel “Danny” Glover is Jethro, with Glover’s phenomenal love and care for everyone around him, and his vocals were not so bad, either. VERDICT: 20/20

# Music

# The score here is absolutely iconic and phenomenal, with it being both powerful and peaceful, as well as knowing how to make you cry, but it is the songs that are real standouts. “Deliver Us” is the opening number that had no reason to go this hard, as it does anything but dumb down the situation of the Jewish people. "All I Ever Wanted" is Moses wondering who he possibly is, with a calm reprise from Queen Tuya. "Through Heaven's Eyes" shows the fun song of Moses being welcomed back into the Jewish culture, by Jethro, his father-in-law by the end of the absolute jam. "Playing with the Big Boys" is Hotep and Huy attempting to detain Moses’s thoughts of attempting to free his people, which is both creepy and fun. "The Plagues" is the most important to the story, and the most heartbreaking, as due to us having spent so much time with the brothers Moses and Rameses. Finally, "When You Believe" is the song that won the Oscar for best original song, and for a good reason, too: it feels so powerful: like a family reunion, basically like a deceased ancestor coming back from the dead. VERDICT: 20/20

# To conclude, The Prince Of Egypt (1998) is a powerful film that many will love to see two years in time, as it gets its rerelease 30 years later, and deservedly so: it is an absolute masterpiece, that will be remembered for years more to come. I would recommend this to any biblical fan, and to anyone else, even atheists! Final Score: 100%
