**Pocahontas (1995)**. The movie was first pitched by Mike Gabriel, the co-director, at a studio “gong show”. Gabriel sketched the Tiger Lily from Peter Pan (1953) alongside forest creatures, with this tagline: An Indian princess torn between her father’s wishes to destroy the English settlers and her wish to help them. Then, in 1992’s June, Jeffrey Katzenberg held a meeting with animators, declaring the movie as the next big budget masterpiece, with The Lion King (1994) being a side project. But not only would they hire consultants, but they would make Pocahontas older in this adaptation. If Beauty And The Beast (1991) was the first ever animated feature to win a Best Picture nomination, Pocahontas would be the first in animation to win the Best Picture Oscar. But by the time it released in theaters, fans and critics alike called it a mess. I personally call this a great movie, and fairly underrated at that. Why? **SPOILERS!
Story**
=======

# If there is one thing about this movie’s writing, it is that it had all of the potential in the world. This film was inspired by projects like West Side Story, Romeo and Juliet, and so much more, but with a native Americans and English settlers aesthetic. Hence, why the story is about how Pocahontas and John Smith are able to learn from each other about their own culture, despite their races being at war. That, and the story’s setting of the new world, the film could handle wonderfully themes of anti-prejudice. So, why did it not? There are two problems for the movie. One, the tone. The tone has not a clue if it wants to be fun for children, or powerful for adults. This gave me tonal whiplash, while watching the movie. A very strange issue, indeed, from Disney. Considering how many of their projects can be fun for both adults and for children. The scenes with Meeko and Percy are made of slapstick, like Tom and Jerry (1940-), and out of nowhere, the settlers kill Kocoum. Two, it can be offensive in the climax. It presents both the natives and settlers as the villains, which did not happen, in real life. Now, while the adult stuff is powerful, and the child stuff is still fun, the pacing cannot put them in the right scenes. This makes the film have a bit too much filler. And, while yes, the film is not historically accurate, are we not talking about Disney? Historical accuracy is not required for this film, let alone the studio. **VERDICT: 12/20**

# **Animation**

# This film is not very acclaimed, in terms of writing, acting or music, but there is no denying that the animation is a spectacle, and an actual work of art. An interesting thing to know is that the crew had to make the film look more realistic than most of the time, to the point where they even cut down most of the Disney aesthetic. This is very much the case with the character designs, where they look like the have received more accuracy, than artistry, giving the characters a more respectable design. Even the animal slapsticky designs give the film an edge of character animation. The film turned the squash and stretch method down, because they want more natural and realistic movements. The backgrounds, though, are actual works of art. They look extremely realistic to actual backgrounds, and have a very lively color pallet. Seriously! This is one of the most beautiful color pallets in a Disney movie!  But the special effects are just otherworldly. Rather it be imagery from smoke, the water being angry or calm, the lighting, or elements blowing in the wind, the list goes on! This is one of those Disney movies that looks so good, you start to forgive its writing. **VERDICT: 20/20**

# **Characters**

# Most of the time, Pocahontas characters’ chemistry is great, but I wish I got more from them as individuals. How so? Pocahontas is a native woman who falls in love with a settler man. That is it. She has no struggles for her father’s approval and no worries to do what he would expect. John Smith as an individual is more moral than the other settlers, and starts to lose their approval, as he becomes a better person. We never see their struggles, even if they have such developed chemistry. Then, I had a revelation. It is not about two people loving each other, but what it means to their families. It is a strange means of peace in a movie of war. This helps the movie feel more human, and mean something to me. There is very little to say about the side characters, as while they are charming, they can be very underdeveloped. Nakoma and Thomas are the best friends of each side, Powhatan is Pocahontas’ father, and the comic relief characters, if pretty funny characters, are Wiggins, Meeko and Percy. The mystical character Grandmother Willow is the mystic figure and Pocahontas’ guide. She fits the natives’ beliefs, and has great quotes. Then, there is Governor Ratcliffe, the titular antagonist. Now, I see where they were going with him. They wanted Ratcliffe to represent the war side. Unfortunately, it failed miserably. This is because it results in Ratcliffe being stereotypical to white people. Now, the characters on average are fine, but I wish there was more to them. **VERDICT: 14/20**

# **Acting**

# Irene Bedard is Pocahontas. Bedard really sounds authentic with a noble presence whenever she steps onto the screen. But she can also have a vulnerable side. This really stops her portrayal from being one dimensional. On top of that, she also has such incredible chemistry, with Mel Gibson‘s John Smith. I did not scream it out, because Gibson was a humongous miscasting. You can really hear him foaming it in, and the effort was just not really there.  Nakoma and Thomas are portrayed by Michelle St. John and Christian Bale. St. John and Bale have such incredible chemistry with Bedard and Gibson. The late activist Russell Means portrays Powhatan. Means could have had a loud, boisterous and powerful portrayal of his character, but he does not do that. Means instead has a more peaceful voice. This perfectly matches his loving relationship with his daughter. John Kassir, Danny Mann and  David Ogden Stiers. Kassir, Mann and Stiers are so humorous throughout the movie.  David Ogden Stiers also plays Ratcliffe, and he sucked in this role. He really foams it in, and has no threatening presence. For example, when he says, “Success will be mine at last”, why did he say it like that? But to make up for that, Linda Hunt as Grandmother Willow has such chemistry with Bedard, and has such power, yet softness in her voice. Some of the actors here, were also cast in Open Season, which came out 11 years after the film, but they did better here. **VERDICT: 18/20**

# **Music**

# The score is just incredible. It really captures the vibe of the movie, and makes you feel like they cared about the project, even if it is not historically accurate. The songs however? Mostly bangers! There are two different styles that affect the tone of the story, and the sides of the characters. The first is from the 1600’s British style, such as “The Virginia Company” and “Mine, Mine, Mine”. They really provoke some thought into who these visitors are, and what they want. Then, there is the traditional nature-connecting Native American style. The songs are “Just Around The River Bend”, “Steady As The Beating Drum” and “Listen To Your Heart”. They perfectly get the side of the Natives’ story. But when you combine the two song styles together, you get both the best and the worst songs in the movie. The worst is “Savages”, because it presents both sides as evil. That is both not true, and racist to those who were protecting their home. Then, for the best song in the movie is “Colors Of The Wind”. “Colors Of The Wind” is a song that deserved the Oscar for Best Original Song for three reasons. One: It’s message of protecting wildlife is important, and very well explored. Two: The animation perfectly compliments the song. Three: It is the pivotal and greatest moment for John Smith’s character development. **VERDICT: 16/20**

# **In conclusion**, Pocahontas (1995) is a great movie, and one I will stand for, against its critics, even if it is not nearly as good as Disney thought it was. Yes, the film has its problems, but with its strengths, it can hold its own. Before you watch this, just do not compare it to other Disney movies of its time. Just have an open mind, and you will have a great time. This is a movie that you must check out if you believe in tradition and do not mind differences between fact and film. **Final Score: 80%**
