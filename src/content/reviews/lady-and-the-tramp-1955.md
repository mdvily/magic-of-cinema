**Lady And The Tramp (1955)**. In the year of 1937, when Disney’s company started releasing films, Joe Grant had a new child of his own. Unfortunately, his cocker spaniel dog was upset. The dog felt like it had been replaced. Grant sent this news public, and Disney used this as an idea for his Studio’s 15th movie. As the the film released as of today, 71 years and 3 days ago, fans disapproved of it, even calling it a bad movie. However, in recent years, the movie has gotten a reevaluation. Fans even calling this a classic, to this day! But is this a good dog, or is this as bad as it was all those years ago? **SPOILERS!**

**Story**

After watching this movie for the first time, I disliked it quite a bit. A reason is because I found the movie predictable, and the pacing slow. I still find the movie predictable. How so? Lady and Tramp get curious of each other. This happened a lot, even at the time. They get separated. This happened a lot, even at the time. They end up together, a family of their own. This happened a lot, even at the time. You start to notice how the movie did not take many risks. It keeps the film from having a real vibe. The film is also debatably racist. I might just be wrong, but just think about it. Also, the comedy is hit or miss. I never wanted to stop watching the film, because of its jokes, but I never really laughed a lot. I just… chuckled at best, and rolled my eyes at worst. This is because I do not approve of its cartoonish slapstick that much. But if you can get past that, then this film has a great story. It is not about the overall story, but about what it does with it. For instance, it does something that most of these movies do not do: it slows the pacing down. Now, I did not like it, first watching it, but I have been on its side, as time progressed. This is because I was not used to it at the time. But now, it works for me, because Disney does not do this a lot. Also, it makes the already two sided romance feel more natural. But my favorite aspect of the movie’s story, with that beautiful pasta segment, is the heart. Because the pacing is slower than you might expect, the film takes its time more to help with the dramatic scenes, which have such powerful moments. For example, Tramp’s fake out death. There are no words, just expressions that say everything. **VERDICT: 16/20**

**Animation**

This movie looks magnificent, in a way Disney has never looked like before. While I loved the animation most of the time, for the last 14 movies, this movie’s has aged the best. First off, the colors are… no words. The film mostly takes shades of brown, gray and green. This works, because the gray and brown scenery represents how the characters are dogs. The green scenery represents the world building. This brings us to the world building. The world building and backgrounds are beautiful, and I love the attention to detail put into them. You then have the action scene, where Lady, Trusty and Jock try to stop Tramp from ending up in the pound. The scene is very intense, and visceral, even a whopping 71 years later! But my favorite part, however is the character animation and designs. You see, this was the first ever film Disney had made to use an animation method we today refer to as CinemaScope. CinemaScope was used to to be able to have the animation squeezed onto a 35mm screen, and unsqueezed afterwards. This works very well in the film’s favor. The movie also used RotoScoping, which is trading over live action. **VERDICT: 20/20**

**Characters**

We have quite some memorable characters here, and the overall cast has aged pretty well. I would never call this the best cast in all of cinema, but I must factor in how well they do work. Lady is our main protagonist. She loves her owners very much, even if she feels upset over her adoptive sister possibly replacing her. Now, as I feel that way occasionally with my younger brother, I do not like her as a character that much. One: she never has a real character arc. Two: her nervousness just disappears as she runs away from home. Tramp is absolutely, though, my favorite character of the entire movie. Why? He is the only one to actually have a character arc. He learns to be more honest, and to cause less mischief. Trusty and Jock are humorous, however, they have no real purpose to the story, and whenever I see them, I want the movie to get back to the story. Jim Dear and Darling are Lady’s masters. The two are the perfect catalysts to the story. This is because they feel real and believable. Finally, little Lulu, is an adorable baby, and important to the story. Aunt Sarah, however, just no. What does she even do in the story, other than yell at Lady? **VERDICT: 15/20**

**Acting**

The acting is very well done, even if there are very little flaws. Barbara Luddy is Lady. Luddy has a real attitude, and understands *perfectly* Lady’s character of a dog, learning more about the outside world. You really get to feel everything you want to feel about Luddy’s portrayal of Lady. Also, she has such incredible chemistry with Larry Roberts as Tramp. Roberts as Tramp is an incredibly big rule breaker, and has such charm and flair. Lee Millar and Peggy Lee are Jim Dear and Darling. They have such chemistry, and their voices are very soft. This makes them welcoming as characters. Bill Baucom and Bill Thompson are Jock and Trusty. The duo has the chemistry with Lady, and each other. Also, their voices give the characters a real vibe. And lastly, Verna Felton as Aunt Sarah is just…
