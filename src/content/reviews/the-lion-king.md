# The Lion King. Disney’s 33rd picture, Pocahontas, was supposed to be the next masterpiece of Disney. The 32nd feature, The Lion King would just be a side project. A forgettable disaster. But that’s not what happened. Instead, this movie became so acclaimed, that it’s in the Library Of Congress, and even the favorite animated film of many! But how good is it? How is it not overrated? SPOILERS! Let’s find out!

# Story

# This film does everything to exist, and I had no idea that I’d love it for existing. Right off the bat, the stakes are real. Simba, our protagonist, makes mistakes, that could potentially, get someone killed. For example, when his uncle Scar, gets him in a dangerous situation, his father Mufasa dies. The film takes advantage of these stakes. We spend so much with the 1st act, with the worldbuilding, that the stakes feel real, and genuine. The second act knows that it’s a family movie, and a fun one at that! It utilizes its setting, and gives off earned character development. The pacing is perfect, the stakes are extremely high, the heart is powerful, the comedy is hilarious, and the theme of learning from your mistakes is executed phenomenally here. VERDICT: 20/20

# Animation

# This film is perfect in animation. The colors are African-Safari themed, in the Timon and Pumbaa paradise, the Pride Lands, and Rafiki’s hut. They’re shaded grey, like how the tone of the scene is. But when it’s a musical number, the colors go all out. While the action scenes in the climax aren’t my favorite part, they’re still very intense, visceral and fluid. The textures look exactly like the scene they’re supposed to look like. The character designs and animation, however, are my favorite part, of the visual spectacle.  5% of the characters look like comedic sidekicks, but the other 95% of the characters have fluid movements that feel like their respective animal. The character designs also get to have human-like faces. They look not weird, but helping us to understand what the characters are thinking. Obeying a golden rule of cinema: “Show, don’t tell,” if you will. VERDICT: 20/20


Characters
==========

# This film has a large cast of characters, so making them stand out was a very difficult task. But it works. For example, we begin with The Lion King himself, Simba. He starts off as an arrogant cub, who has not a care in the world for responsibility. He cares in the second act, but in the way, where he wants to not do anything to do with it. But in one of the greatest scenes of all time, his father in the clouds tells him, that he must return to his responsibilities. He feels ashamed, and when he faces Scar, it feels earned, like he’s a different character. Mufasa, his father, is wise, and delivers powerful philosophies to his son. This is a pivotal part of Simba’s arc. Scar, his uncle is a calculating villain, who charms me every time. I love how, not only is his final confrontation with his nephew the perfect character arc completion, but Disney collaborated with directors Roger Allers, and Rob Minkoff, to have him succeed for most of the movie. Nala, Simba’s love interest has a slight character arc of bravery, and a flamboyant personality, plus she’s a great role model to women, so I love her regardless. Timon and Pumbaa are hilarious, but they learn to use their comedy for the greater good, not just to be funny. Rafiki is basically the prophet of the story, who’s a funnier version of Yoda, and the tiniest bit less wise. Zazu is Mufasa’s advisor, with humorous logic. Banzai, Shenzi and Ed are all hilarious, yet terrifying, and Sarabi, or Simba’s mother, is just badass. VERDICT: 20/20

# Acting

# These actors perfectly get into the characters’ heads and give it their all. Matthew Broderick as Simba is sarcastic, yet charming. Many people think his portrayal was off, but I disagree. James Earl Jones as Mufasa gives his character wisdom. Jeremy Irons as Scar is sadistic, flamboyant, ambitious, and delightful. Like a very evil red velvet cake. Moria Kelly puts the concern, yet snappiness into Nala. Nathan Lane, Ernie Sabella, and Rowan “Mr. Bean” Atkinson as Timon, Pumbaa and Zazu are hillarious. Robert Guillaume as Rafiki is funny, yet wise. Whoopi Goldberg, Cheech Martin and Jim Cunnings as the hyenas are full of life. Madge Sinclair puts in maternal power as Sarabi. VERDICT: 20/20

# Music

# Every song perfectly affects the scene it’s in, and has an appropriate level of buildup. There are only 5, but there are 5 pitch-perfect bangers. Circle Of Life is the secondary message, of how we must all take out places in the circle of life. It has excellent lyrics, and perfect vocals. I Just Can’t Wait To Be King is a catchy song, that perfectly emphasizes Simba’s motivation. Be Prepared has not the most people talking about it, so it deserves to be called underrated. It has amazing vocals from Irons, and is a rare instance of how telling, not showing, can sometimes work, albeit, rarely. Hakuna Matata makes the meerkat and warthog duo famous, due to its significance to the story, and catchiness. Can You Feel The Love Tonight? is a love story, where we don’t just get read into Simba’s perspective, but Nala’s too. This gives it an appropriate level of depth. But the score? This was the first animated film score, of Hans Zimmer’s career, and potentially the greatest score of all time. My personal favorite, because every beat, every tune, fits the scene perfectly. VERDICT: 20/20

# This is one of the best films ever made, in my opinion. As the first film, I ever watched, it got me into movies, in the best possible way. No words could ever do it justice, and there’s so much to say about it. However, what hasn’t already been said about this masterpiece. I’m sure, everyone’s watched it. The story is cohesive, from beginning to end, the animation  looks beautiful, and otherworldly in every frame, every character is inspirational, both in terms of writing and acting, and the music is a forever delight for me to listen to. Final Score: 100%
