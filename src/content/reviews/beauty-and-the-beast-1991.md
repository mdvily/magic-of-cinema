**Beauty And The Beast (1991)**.Once upon a time, Disney animation studios was facing a crisis. The founder of the studio, Walter “Walt” Disney passed away, and the animators crew, the nine old men lived up to their name and retired. Therefore, as a result, the Disney movies quality slipped from great to pretty bad, from The Aristocrats (1970) and Oliver and Company (1988). Yes, with The Great Mouse Detectives (1986) and The Rescuers (1978), there were some good additions to the Walt Disney Pictures family, but it was not the same. However, Disney did what they did best in terms of storytelling, and new technology into their animation into The Little Mermaid (1989) and The Rescuers Down Under (1990), bringing the studio back to its roots. However, Beauty And The Beast (1991) of the original three Disney Renaissance is considered one of the most acclaimed, with an 8 on IMDb, and a 95% on rotten tomatoes, the film is considered one of the greatest in not just Disney, but all of animation, even being the first ever animated feature to win a Best Picture nomination. But is this a masterpiece as old as time? SPOILERS!

**Story**

As an animated feature based on a French Fairy Tale by Gabrielle-Suzanne Barbot de Villeneuve in 1740, the film does what Disney does best: deviating from the tale 251 years before the picture. And sure enough: it works in the movie’s favor!

**Acting**

Robby Benson is Beast, with such finesse and a change in tone. At the start of the movie, Benson has such an attitude, and callousness to his voice, but in the end, he sounds way more nice to Belle, perfectly reflecting upon Beast’s character. Paige O'Hara as Belle is incredibly sweet, yet knows when to have an attitude, when needed, all while having a beautiful singing voice. Jerry Orbach as Lumiere has such a charming French voice, yet knows when to have a more serious tone. Angela Lansbury as Mrs. Potts has such amazing chemistry and motherly tone with Bradley Pierce as Chip Potts. David Ogden Stiers as Cogsworth sounds more hard on Beast, and far more skeptical of Belle, which perfectly makes sense for his character. Richard White as Gaston
