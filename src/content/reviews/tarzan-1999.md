# **Tarzan (1999)**. Disney has created many features, but up until June 12, 1999, they had never made a movie about the Ape-Man himself. Based on the Edgar Rice Burroughs novel in 1912, Disney wanted to make a film about a man, known for his signature yell, loincloth as his only clothing, and his connection with animals. As the final movie of arguably the most acclaimed Disney era of all time, the creators of the movie took no chances, and studied how the animals would move and act, in Africa. They also used skateboarder Tony Hawk’s movements to animate Tarzan as a “tree surfer”. The film is considered quite a good film, and one of Disney’s most heartfelt, with a 90% on Rotten Tomatoes. But is it so, or should I just team up with Clayton to hunt the gorillas? **SPOILERS!**

# **Story**

# As a Disney adaptation, the movie deviates from the 1912 book, in order to tell its own story. This movie has occasionally led to debates if the writers even cared about the original, sure, it was very necessary. This means that Disney has a real vibe with its story. It follows Tarzan, not trying to win his family’s approval, but to know where he belongs. Should he stay with the gorillas, or return home, with the humans? It really makes the film stand out in a sea of Tarzan adaptations, and not just making it, “The Tarzan Movie With Phil Collins”. It also helps that the pacing knows how to take its time with a scene, and then jump to the next, naturally. From the very start, the movie wants us to know that it cares most about its heart. Tarzan (1999) wants to make you feel for the character and actually tear up, for some scenes. This especially helps when Kala first meets Tarzan, and saves him from Sabor. However, they never tell you anything for that scene, because they trust you to understand what goes on. Another heartfelt scene is when Tarzan puts on human clothes for the first time, and says no matter where he goes, Kala will always be his mother. This makes for a heartfelt and calm scene. And since the film spends so much time with the gorillas in the first act, it hits even harder. Now, this does drag me to my main criticism of the movie: the second half makes this a completely different movie. Now, it still worked for me, and I still was invested, but I expect it to be more fitting to the first act. Now, this means that the gorillas did get noticeably sidelined. My other criticism is the comedy. Do not get me wrong, this is a pretty funny movie, but I wish that the comedy was more prominent. But if you can get past that, the story is absolutely amazing. **VERDICT: 18/20**

# **Animation**

# As the 1990’s progressed, Disney was getting into more and more 3D computer animation. Yet, by the time they started working on Tarzan, CG started to become extremely prominent in their animated features, as much as the hand drawn projects. For this movie, they did the something for the first time: use an animation process we refer today to as Deep Canvas. This allowed the team to create 3D backgrounds, and make them look 2D animated. The film looked revolutionary in the final year of the 90’s, to the point where this would even be applied for later Disney movies, such as Treasure Planet (2002). It would even be like the prototype, leading to more stylized looks, such as Spider-Man: Into and Across the Spider-Verse (2018 and 2023), Puss in Boots: The Last Wish (2022) and Teenage Mutant Ninja Turtles: Mutant Mayhem (2023). And looking back at it today, I would say that is has stood the test of time. Especially with the effects of water and the jungle trees falling down, before Tarzan first sees the humans. Every single background succeeds in terms of making the world feel massive, applying Tarzan all the space he needs to move around the jungle. I also love the color pallet, and how it mostly sticks to green. This showcases how massive the world is. The landscapes also provide great attention to detail. This makes for some insane accuracy to African jungles. The character designs and animation is very accurate to real life. Not once did I see a movement and start to think, “Yeah, they just put that in there.” But my favorite aspect of a beautiful film is the cinematography and action sequences. First the cinematography. I not only was able to follow Tarzan as he navigated throughout the area, but felt like I was on a roller coaster ride. Lastly, the action sequences. They get very intense, without having to be too violent for a G-rated movie. **VERDICT: 20/20**

# **Characters**

# The jungles of Tarzan is absolutely massive, and I guess I can say the same thing about its eight main characters. But the quantity does not matter to me if I do not like the quality. Fortunately, that is not the case here. Tarzan is our main protagonist. Most of his adaptations are him trying to fit in his family. What I love about him here is that he instead wants to know where he belongs. This gives him a real aesthetic and makes him a very original version of himself. Jane Porter is his love interest. Now, they could have just made her vulnerable to Tarzan, but they did not do that. Instead, they make her fascinated to learn about who this stranger is, but can be disgusted at some of Tarzan’s antics. This makes her very complex, and I love how they did not make her a Disney princess. Kala is Tarzan’s adoptive mother. She is the anchor for the more emotional and heartfelt scenes, and she is the perfect metaphor of Tarzan’s aspirations to stay. Terk and Tantor are Tarzan’s friends. Along the movie, Terk learns to be less cynical of Tarzan, and Tantor faces his fears more. They are very funny, sure, but I wish that they served more of a purpose to the story. Kerchak is the silverback patriarch of the gorillas. In the book, he is the main antagonist, but in the movie, he is the leader who is concerned of Tarzan, and gets more and more of a heart towards his adoptive son. Clayton is the villain of the movie. While he just wants to make a couple bucks, he still could have been worse. This is because he is a good and competent foil to Tarzan, and his death is the stuff of nightmares. Dr. Porter is Jane’s curious father. He is my least favorite character, because he belongs on the story the least. I mean, take him out of it. What would have changed? **VERDICT: 16/20**

# **Acting**

# Last watching this film, I thought I would even remotely pick up a criticism for acting, but that never happened. I thought that the acting was phenomenal. Tony Goldwyn (Voice) and Bob Bergen (Yell) are Tarzan. Bergen’s yell is iconic, and he really sells it, but Goldwyn does most of the voice acting, so he gets most of the spotlight. Goldwyn really can sell the emotion of Tarzan, but he also has a lot of kindness and curiosity in his voice. Since Tarzan himself has these character traits, this works in the favor of Goldwyn’s portrayal of the Ape-Man. Minnie Driver is Jane. Driver has a lot of kindness in her voice, as well as curiosity about Tarzan. You really buy how she wants to learn more about the man, but you also feel how she can be disgusted by some of the things he does, like tickling her toes after he saves her. On top of that, she has great chemistry with Nigel Hawthorne as Dr. Porter. Glenn Close is Kala, and this is my favorite animated role of hers. Close really knows how to tug your heart strings. She delivers her lines with such sincerity. Lance Henriksen is Kerchak. Henriksen sounds so cynical, but you buy it, because that is in his character. He also knows how to sound soft, such as when he dies, officially acknowledging Tarzan as his son. Rosie O'Donnell and Wayne Knight are Terk and Tantor. O’Donnell and Knight are hilarious as the two, but can sound very natural when they speak. Yes, I wish that they could have serious moments, but that is not their fault. Lastly, Brian Blessed is Clayton. Blessed can sound very menacing, but he still has his smooth charm. **VERDICT: 20/20**

# **Music**

# I have to give huge praise to the score composer Mark Mancina. Mancina delivered perfectly on the atmospheric and authentic tracks. “Moves Like An Ape, Looks Like A Man” is a lighthearted tune, I had fun listening to. “The Gorillas” and “A Wondrous Place” help with the climactic tune. But then you have the Phil Collin’s soundtrack, which made the movie, well, the movie. “Two Worlds” is the heartfelt opening. Collins went hard with the film’s main anthem. “You'll Be In My Heart” is a more heartfelt song, which showcases the best aspect. “Son Of Man” is the most important part of the first act, and the lyrics care a lot about it! “Trashin' The Camp” is the only original song from the movie. I hate it because it is annoying, and had no purpose in the story. “Strangers Like Me” is the makeup song, however, and it is my favorite. It really builds up excitement for what will later happen, and the lyrics are amazing! **VERDICT: 18/20**

# **In conclusion**, Tarzan is an absolute masterpiece. Yes, it can have issues with some of its writing, but it makes up for it, with a cohesive story, breathtaking animation, lovable characters, great acting and a banger score and soundtrack. As the final Disney Renaissance animated movie, while this is one of their weakest, it is still a very enjoyable experience. It would then come as no surprise that I would recommend this to any avid fan of the 1912 novel needs to check out. **Final Score: 92%**
